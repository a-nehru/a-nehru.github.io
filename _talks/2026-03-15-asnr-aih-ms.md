**Background**

Motor impairment is a primary consequence of multiple sclerosis (MS), yet therapies to restore function remain limited. Acute intermittent hypoxia (AIH) enhances neuroplasticity and motor function in individuals with spinal cord injury and stroke via serotonin and brain-derived neurotrophic factor-dependent mechanisms. The efficacy of AIH in MS remained unstudied.

**Methods**

Twenty participants with MS (EDSS 5.4±1.2, age 59.3±11.7 years) completed a randomized crossover trial comparing AIH versus sham. The AIH protocol: 15 bouts of alternating hypoxia (60s at 9% O₂) and normoxia (60s at 21% O₂) daily for five consecutive days. Ankle plantarflexion and dorsiflexion MVCs were measured via Biodex dynamometer at baseline, post-intervention (day 5), and one-week follow-up.

**Results**

- Plantarflexion torque: **+12%** post-intervention, **+30%** at follow-up (p < 0.05)
- Dorsiflexion torque: **+24%** post-intervention, **+27%** at follow-up (p < 0.05)
- No significant changes in sham condition or EMG activity

**Conclusions**

Daily AIH elicits sustained increases in volitional strength in people with MS, supporting its role as a primer for combinatorial rehabilitation interventions.
