**Timeline:** 2018–2023  

### **The Beginning: A Simple Question**
Hand therapy after neurological injuries like **stroke** often requires training a wide range of movements at various hand joints. During my doctoral studies, I was driven by a fundamental question:  
*Can a simple, single-degree-of-freedom robotic system effectively train multiple hand functions? Could this simplicity eventually enhance usage and increase therapy dosage?*  

This question stemmed from a pressing need in rehabilitation—creating devices that are compact, affordable, and relevant to clinical and home settings, especially in resource-limited environments. Complex robots, while advanced, often lack accessibility and cost-effectiveness. **PLUTO** was my answer to this challenge, designed to bridge the gap between cutting-edge technology and real-world usability.

---

### **System Architecture: Modular and Adaptive**
At the heart of **PLUTO** is its simplicity and adaptability. The system was inspired by a kitchen blender—a **single actuator** with **modular passive mechanisms** that can be easily attached and detached. These mechanisms allow the robot to target specific hand functions while maintaining a compact and user-friendly design.  

The current version of PLUTO supports training for the following five hand functions:  
1. **Wrist flexion/extension (WFE)**  
2. **Wrist ulnar/radial deviation (WURD)**  
3. **Forearm pronation/supination (FPS)**  
4. **Gross hand opening and closing (HOC)**  
5. **Functional handles** for task-oriented training.  

---

### **Clinical Impact: From Usability to Feasibility**
Over its development, PLUTO went through **four iterations**, each refined using a **user-centric design approach**. Feedback from over **200 stakeholders**, including patients, clinicians, and caregivers, was integral to the process. This approach ensured the device was intuitive, easy to set up, and suitable for **independent use**.

One of the highlights of this journey was a **four-week home study**, where patients used PLUTO independently with minimal assistance from caregivers. A particularly memorable moment came from a patient in a **remote tribal village**, 150 kilometers from the nearest hospital, who successfully used PLUTO on their own. This reinforced the importance of creating affordable and accessible solutions that truly reach those who need them the most.

These experiences transformed what began as a simple usability test into a profound learning opportunity, demonstrating the potential of PLUTO to bring meaningful change to rehabilitation.

---

### **What I Learned**
PLUTO is more than a robot—it’s a reflection of my journey as an engineer and my commitment to creating impactful solutions. Through its development, I realized the **true value of simplicity and accessibility in technology**. Innovation is not just about building advanced systems; it’s about ensuring those systems can make a tangible difference in people’s lives.  

As of now, PLUTO is used at **six centers across India**, with its **commercial release** planned for **May 2025**. Its journey reaffirms the significance of thoughtful design in addressing real-world challenges in rehabilitation.

---

### **Contributions and Achievements**
- Conceptualized and developed PLUTO as a **single-degree-of-freedom modular system**.  
- Designed **pluggable passive mechanisms** to train five isolated hand functions.  
- Conducted **usability studies** and **home-based trials**, gaining firsthand insights into patient needs and experiences.  

After completing my PhD, I worked for a year to **transfer PLUTO’s technology to a startup**, focusing on industrial design improvements and ensuring **medical compliance**. Now, PLUTO is ready for commercial launch by **Thryv Rehab Technologies**.
