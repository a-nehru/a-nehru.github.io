**Background**

Motor impairment is a primary consequence of multiple sclerosis (MS), yet therapies to restore function remain limited. Acute intermittent hypoxia (AIH) enhances neuroplasticity and motor function in individuals with spinal cord injury and stroke via serotonin and brain-derived neurotrophic factor-dependent mechanisms. However, the efficacy of AIH protocols in people with MS remains understudied.

**Objective**

To determine the efficacy of daily AIH for improving ankle strength in people with MS.

**Methods**

Twenty participants with MS (EDSS 5.4±1.2, age 59.3±11.7 years, 9 male) completed a randomized crossover trial comparing AIH versus sham intervention. The AIH protocol consisted of 15 bouts of alternating hypoxia (60 seconds at 9% O₂) and normoxia (60 seconds at 21% O₂) delivered over 30 minutes daily for five consecutive days. Maximal voluntary contractions (MVC) during ankle plantarflexion and dorsiflexion were measured using a Biodex dynamometer, along with surface electromyography (EMG), at baseline (day 1), immediately post-intervention (day 5), and at one-week follow-up. Statistical significance was set at p < 0.05.

**Results**

Following daily AIH, plantarflexion torque increased by 12% immediately post-intervention and 30% at one-week follow-up (p < 0.05). Dorsiflexion torque increased by 24% post-intervention and 27% at follow-up (p < 0.05). No significant changes were observed in the sham condition or EMG activity in either group.

**Conclusions**

Daily AIH elicits sustained increases in volitional strength output in individuals with MS, similar to effects observed in populations with spinal cord injury and stroke. These findings support AIH as a promising therapeutic tool to induce plasticity and enhance motor function in people with MS, potentially serving as a primer for combinatorial rehabilitation interventions.
