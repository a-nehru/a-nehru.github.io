## The Problem

Up to 80% of people with multiple sclerosis (MS) develop walking difficulties that significantly restrict everyday participation. Four interconnected neurophysiological impairments drive this:

1. **Weakness** — lower-limb strength reduced 40–70% compared to healthy controls
2. **Fatigue** — affects 90% of PwMS; increased metabolic cost of walking (76% higher O₂ consumption)
3. **Abnormal coactivation** — excessive antagonist muscle co-firing increases joint stiffness and energy cost
4. **Proprioceptive deficits** — demyelinating lesions disrupt sensorimotor integration, degrading gait stability

Conventional rehabilitation approaches are constrained by low dosage, lack of specificity, and poor engagement. Pharmacotherapies can slow disease progression but do not reverse established motor deficits.

---

## The Approach: AIH + MINT

This project proposes a **combinatorial intervention** targeting two complementary mechanisms:

### Acute Intermittent Hypoxia (AIH)
Brief, repetitive episodes of low-oxygen breathing (9% O₂, 60s on / 60s off × 15 cycles, 30 min/day) trigger **global neuroplasticity** via serotonin-dependent upregulation of BDNF, increasing motoneuron excitability and fatigue resistance across the nervous system.

### Myoelectric Interface Neurorehabilitation Training (MINT)
A gamified EMG biofeedback paradigm using wearable surface EMG sensors on agonist-antagonist muscle pairs. Real-time muscle signals control an on-screen cursor — coactivation produces diagonal (off-target) movement, while selective activation drives the cursor straight to the target. Through operant conditioning, participants learn to **suppress pathological antagonist coactivation**. In stroke, MINT has achieved a 70% reduction in coactivation and 0.15 m/s improvement in gait speed.

### Synergy
AIH elevates "plasticity readiness" globally; MINT then drives activity-dependent, task-specific consolidation in the same motor neurons. The two pathways converge to produce **supra-additive BDNF upregulation** in task-relevant neural circuits, expected to yield greater and longer-lasting functional gains than either intervention alone.

**Central hypothesis:** AIH-primed MINT will yield superior and longer-lasting improvements in lower-limb strength, intermuscular coordination, gait performance, and fatigue resistance compared to MINT alone.

## Pilot Data

Preliminary data from our lab (PwMS n=10, controls n=5) confirm pathologically elevated coactivation in MS:

| Direction | Controls | PwMS | Increase |
|-----------|----------|------|----------|
| Dorsiflexion | 100 ± 12% | 163 ± 21% | **+63%** |
| Plantarflexion | 67 ± 15% | 132 ± 28% | **+65%** |

Correspondingly, maximal voluntary torque was markedly reduced: plantarflexion 65 ± 11 vs 99 ± 12 Nm; dorsiflexion 25.7 ± 6.1 vs 45 ± 21 Nm.

## Study Design

**Double-blind, randomized controlled trial** — n = 22 ambulatory adults with MS (EDSS 3.0–7.0), randomized 1:1 to:
- **AIH-primed MINT:** 30 min AIH (9–10% O₂) + 60 min MINT daily × 5 days
- **Sham-primed MINT:** 30 min sham (21% O₂) + 60 min MINT daily × 5 days

**Assessments at 4 time points:** Baseline (Day 1), post-intervention (Day 5), 1-week follow-up, 1-month follow-up.

---

## Three Aims

**Aim 1 — Behavioral Gains**  
Six-Minute Walk Test and Timed 10-Meter Walk Test to determine whether AIH-primed MINT produces additional clinically meaningful improvements in gait endurance and speed.

**Aim 2 — Neuromuscular Changes**  
Biodex dynamometry and surface EMG to measure changes in maximal voluntary torque and agonist-antagonist coactivation (isometric and during walking), determining whether AIH enhances MINT's ability to restore normal muscle activation.

**Aim 3 — Fatigability and Training Capacity**  
Performance fatigability (% torque decline over 20 MVCs) and MINT training volume (repetitions, gameplay time) to determine whether AIH enhances fatigue resistance and enables greater motor learning engagement.

## MINT Training Details

Each 60-minute MINT session comprises six 10-minute runs (~30 cursor-to-target trials/run; ~900 movement attempts/week). Two modules target distinct anatomical levels:

- **Distal module** — tibialis anterior (dorsiflexor) vs. soleus/gastrocnemius (plantarflexors); targets promote dorsiflexion bursts and plantarflexor inhibition during swing phase
- **Proximal module** — rectus femoris (knee extensor) vs. biceps femoris (knee flexor); targets emulate muscle recruitment patterns during knee flexion and stance-to-swing transitions

Adaptive difficulty modulates five parameters in real-time based on accuracy (>80% → harder; <50% → easier), maintaining optimal challenge throughout.

---

## Impact

This work will establish the **first empirical evidence for combining neuromodulation with targeted biofeedback training in MS rehabilitation**. The portable equipment (pulse oximeter-controlled gas delivery for AIH; wireless EMG sensors for MINT) is designed for deployment in outpatient settings and, ultimately, home-based rehabilitation — directly supporting the PVA mission to improve outcomes for the ~60,000 US veterans living with MS.
